Moving scams cost Massachusetts residents thousands in surprise fees, damaged goods, and delayed deliveries. Boston’s tight timelines and September turnover make stress—and fraud—more likely. Know these ten schemes before you hire anyone to touch your belongings.

## 1. The too-good-to-be-true quote

A $399 whole-apartment move in Back Bay should raise alarms. Scammers lowball to win the job, then inflate hours or add fees on site. Get written estimates after inventory from [licensed local movers](/local-moving) and compare [realistic 2026 pricing](/blog/moving-cost-boston-ma-2026).

## 2. Unlicensed “labor only” on Craigslist

Random crews may lack insurance, training, and accountability if something breaks—or disappears. Verify DPU licensing for intrastate work and ask for a COI for your building.

## 3. Name-changing brokers

Some national lead sites sell your job to the lowest bidder. The truck that arrives may not match the brand you researched. Hire the **actual carrier** performing the move; confirm USDOT/MC numbers for interstate jobs on FMCSA’s database.

## 4. Hostage loads and cash demands

If movers refuse to unload until you pay far above estimate—in cash—that is extortion. Prevent it with binding or not-to-exceed terms, documented inventory, and payment rules in writing. Walk away from movers who insist on 100% cash upfront.

## 5. Missing or vague contracts

Never sign blank forms or contracts without company name, address, and rate structure. For long-distance moves, understand delivery windows and storage fees if dates slip.

## 6. Fake insurance promises

“Fully insured” is meaningless without details. Know released value vs full-value protection. Ask for certificate of insurance and what deductible applies.

## 7. Volume or weight bait on interstate moves

Non-binding estimates can balloon if weight exceeds estimate. Scammers under-estimate intentionally. Request binding estimates when possible and be present for weigh-in if applicable.

## 8. Upfront “reservation” fees that vanish

Some fraudsters take deposits then ghost. Use credit cards or checks to reputable businesses with traceable addresses in MA—not wire transfers or gift cards.

## 9. Holding your items in mystery storage

Unclear storage charges rack up daily. Get storage rates, location, and release terms in writing before authorizing storage in transit.

## 10. Identity and phishing follow-ups

After you request quotes online, scammers may email fake payment links. Confirm phone numbers match the company website; pay through official channels only.

## Safer hiring checklist

1. Match company name on truck, contract, and insurance.
2. Read recent reviews on multiple platforms.
3. Use video or in-home surveys—not bedroom-count guesses.
4. Avoid huge cash deposits.
5. Keep valuables and documents with you.
6. Photograph high-value items before load.
